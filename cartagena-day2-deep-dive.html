02 / Street Food Tour – The Tastings

What You’ll Eat

Ten stops, ten tastings. The tour weaves through the Old Town and Getseманí, hitting vendors and hole-in-the-wall spots that locals have been eating at for decades.

set_meal

Ceviche & Patacón con Suero

Coctelería y Cevicería Erika • 50+ years

Colombian-style shrimp ceviche – bolder than Peruvian, with mayo, ketchup, and lime juice. Served alongside patacón con suero: smashed fried plantain topped with fresh white cheese. Washed down with Kola Román, Cartagena’s iconic cherry soda.

The spot: Named after the owner’s daughter. Operating for over 50 years. The kind of place that doesn’t need a sign – everyone knows it.

egg_alt

Arepa de Huevo

Caribbean Coast Speciality

The queen of Cartagena street food. A corn arepa is fried once, split open, a whole egg is cracked inside, then it’s sealed and fried again until golden and crispy. The egg steams inside the dough. Served with aji (hot sauce). You’ll also try carimañola (yuca dough stuffed with meat) and papa rellena (stuffed potato).

Technique: Watch the vendor work – the double-fry method is mesmerising. The timing of the egg seal has to be perfect or it leaks.

cake

Portal de los Dulces

Plaza de los Coches • Centuries-old sweet market

An arcaded corridor on Plaza de los Coches lined with confectionery stands selling homemade Caribbean sweets. You’ll try alegrías – chewy, sticky squares of caramelised coconut – plus cocadas (coconut candy), dulce de leche, and passion fruit and guava sweets.

History: The alegría recipe was brought from Africa over 400 years ago. The plaza itself was once a slave market during the Spanish empire – where Africans were sold to plantation owners. The sweet vendors reclaimed the space. The name “alegría” means “joy.”

nutrition

Exotic Fruits & Juices

Caribbean fruit extracts

Colombia has more fruit varieties than almost anywhere on earth. You’ll try fresh tropical juices – lulo, maracuyá (passion fruit), guanabana (soursop), corozo – most of which don’t exist outside of South America. Paired with pandebono: warm, chewy cheese bread made from yuca flour and queso fresco.

Pandebono: Originally from Valle del Cauca. The best ones are warm from the oven – crispy outside, soft and cheesy inside. The street vendors make them fresh all day.

soup_kitchen

Traditional Cartagena Soup

Local comedor

A hearty Caribbean soup at a local comedor (family-run eatery). Cartagena’s soup traditions blend Indigenous, African, and Spanish influences – expect rich broths with yuca, plantain, and fresh seafood.

local_cafe

Craft Beer & Colombian Coffee

Final stops • Getseманí

The tour winds down in Getseманí with a local craft beer and a proper Colombian coffee. Colombia produces some of the world’s best arabica beans, and the guide explains the difference between what gets exported and what Colombians actually drink.

03 / Castillo de San Felipe de Barajas schedule ~16:30

The Largest Spanish Fortress in the Americas

Built 1639–1767 on the Hill of San Lázaro to defend against pirates and rival empires. Underground tunnels, massive ramparts, 68 cannons, and panoramic views over the city and harbour. UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1984. Never successfully taken by siege.

The Tunnel System

Underground gallery • Sea-level perimeter

The main underground gallery runs along the entire perimeter of the complex at sea level. Tunnels were designed with acoustic engineering – sounds carry so defenders could hear approaching enemies. Some chambers were booby-trapped: packed with explosives to detonate and collapse the ground under advancing attackers.

Tip: The audio guide (+COP 15,000 / ~£3) is worth it specifically for the tunnel sections – it explains the acoustic design and military logic. Bring a phone torch for the darker passages.

castle

Ramparts & Batteries

8 battery positions • Zigzagging ramps

Eight battery positions (Santa Bárbara, San Carlos, Los Apóstoles, Hornabeque, La Cruz, La Redención, San Lázaro, and the central castle) with overlapping fields of fire. The batteries protect one another – making it practically impossible to take one without taking the entire system. Zigzagging ramps were designed to disorient attackers.

Views: The upper ramparts give panoramic views over Cartagena, the harbour, and the approach routes from sea and land. At 4:30–5:30 PM you get golden-hour light on the stone – the best time for photos.

architecture

Defensive Architecture

Antonio de Arévalo • Triangular design

Built in a triangular shape on the hilltop. Walls are wide at the base and narrow toward the parapet, forming an interlocking system of bunkers. The fortress was designed by military engineer Antonio de Arévalo and named in honour of Philip IV of Spain. Built by African slave labour under Spanish supervision.

04 / Castillo – Historical Context

The Battle of Cartagena de Indias, 1741

The defining moment in the fortress’s history. Britain’s worst military defeat before the 20th century – and the reason San Felipe still stands. Your audio guide will cover this, but knowing the scale beforehand makes the ramparts hit differently.

🇬🇧 British Invasion Force

Ships 186
Cannons 2,620

Sailors 12,600
Colonial Recruits 4,000 (Virginia)

🇪🇸 Spanish Defenders

Ships 6
Total Defenders 3,000–6,000
Includes Regular troops, militia, native archers
Commander Admiral Blas de Lezo

Blas de Lezo: One-eyed, one-armed, one-legged. Lost his left leg at 15 (Battle of Málaga), his left eye at 25, and his right arm at 31. Still commanded the defence of Cartagena at 52. Known as “Mediohombre” – Half-Man.

67 Days That Decided an Empire

Mar
13 March – British Fleet Arrives

Admiral Vernon arrives with 186 ships – the largest amphibious assault force Britain had ever assembled. More ships than the Spanish Armada. So confident of victory that he had commemorative medals struck in advance showing Blas de Lezo kneeling in surrender.

Apr
De Lezo’s Strategy: Delay Until the Rains

Outnumbered 6-to-1, de Lezo knew he couldn’t win a direct battle. His plan: fighting withdrawal, delay, and attrition. Keep the British crowded on ships and exposed on land until the rainy season hit in late April. Tropical disease would do the rest.

20 Apr
The Assault on San Felipe

British troops storm the fortress at night with scaling ladders. But the ladders are too short – they can’t reach the top of the walls. Spanish defenders pour boiling tar and musket fire from above. The assault collapses into chaos. Hundreds killed in the moat.

May
20 May – British Withdraw

After 67 days, the rains arrive. Tropical disease tears through the fleet. Vernon orders retreat. British losses: ~15,000 dead, 1,500 cannons, 50 ships lost or damaged. Of the 4,000 Virginia colonists, only 300 returned home. Spanish losses: ~800 men.

history_edu The Aftermath

Vernon’s medals: Before the battle, Vernon was so certain of victory that he had commemorative medals minted showing Blas de Lezo on his knees. They were already circulating in London. The humiliation of defeat was compounded by the medals’ existence – the British government attempted to suppress knowledge of the battle entirely.

De Lezo’s fate: Despite the victory, Blas de Lezo died weeks later from plague caused by the unburied dead. He was 52. There is now a statue of him outside the fortress walls.

Strategic impact: The defeat ended Britain’s ambitions to control the Caribbean trade routes through Cartagena. Spain maintained its hold on the port for another 80 years.

Among the colonial recruits: Lawrence Washington, half-brother of George Washington, served in Vernon’s fleet. He survived and later named his Virginia estate “Mount Vernon” after the admiral. Without the defeat at Cartagena, American history might look very different.

05 / After the Fort schedule ~18:30

Walk Back Through Getseманí

Instead of a taxi back, walk 20 minutes from the fortress through Getseманí to catch the street art murals in the golden-hour light. This is Cartagena’s most bohemian neighbourhood – the murals tell stories of Afro-Colombian heritage, resistance, and identity.

palette

Calle de la Sierpe

“Serpent Street” • Epicentre of Cartagena street art

The winding “Serpent Street” is the epicentre of wall art in the city. The most photographed piece is the Grinning Afro-Colombian Woman by artist Dexs – a large, striking mural paying homage to Cartagena’s African heritage. The street is an uninterrupted corridor of colour.

Route: From the fortress, walk via Calle del Arsenal into Getseманí. Turn onto Calle 29 (Calle Sierpe). Continue to Callejón Angosto for more murals.

photo_camera

Callejón Angosto & Surrounds

Calle de las Sombrillas • Plaza de la Trinidad

Continue to Callejón Angosto for more murals, then find Calle de las Sombrillas – overhung with colourful umbrellas that light up at night. End at Plaza de la Trinidad, the heart of Getseманí, where locals gather in the evening. The Maria Mulata sculpture sits in the square.

Timing: Walking back from San Felipe at ~6:30 PM puts you in Getseманí as the light softens. The murals look best in late-afternoon/golden-hour light. Budget 20–30 min for the walk if you stop for photos.
