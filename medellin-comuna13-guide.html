03 / Comuna 13 – Key Stops

Escalators, Murals, Performers & Street Food

Everything is concentrated along the escalator corridor. You ride up through the heart of the neighbourhood, surrounded by art on every surface.

escalator

The Outdoor Escalators (Escaleras Eléctricas)

World first • Installed 2011 • Free to ride
Sections 6 covered outdoor escalators
Total length 1,260 ft (384 metres)
Replaces ~350 concrete steps
Time saved 30-min climb → 5–6 min ride
New public space 1,102 sq metres created
Cost Free

The world’s first outdoor public escalators in a residential area. The concept has since been copied by Rio de Janeiro and Ankara.

Significance: They physically connected an isolated, stigmatised community to the rest of Medellín. Residents could now access jobs, schools, and services without the gruelling daily climb.

Experience: You ride up through the heart of the neighbourhood, surrounded by murals and street art on every surface. Each section deposits you at a small plaza before the next escalator begins. Street vendors, performers, and art stalls line the route.

brush

Notable Murals & Their Stories

The murals are not just decoration–they are community memory, healing, and resistance.

Operación Orión Mural by Chota / @chota13

The most significant mural in Comuna 13. Depicts an African-Colombian girl alongside another girl with tears on her face, mourning the victims of the 2002 military operation. Plants growing around the figures symbolise the community’s resilience and hope for a brighter future.

Artist: Chota (John Alexander Serna)

The most influential street artist of Comuna 13. Grew up in the sector of Independencias I when urban militias controlled the area. Created most of the major murals. His work documents the community’s trauma and transformation.

landscape

Viewpoints (Miradores)

Mirador de la Comuna 13: The main observation platform at the top of the escalator route. Uninterrupted panorama of Medellín’s urban landscape stretching across the Aburrá Valley. Open daily 9AM–5PM.

The Viaducto: A walking path above the neighbourhood lined with shops, street food vendors, and performers. Good second viewpoint.

Casa Neon: Interactive art gallery with neon paintings on the walls. Visitors get 3D glasses to view the art. Donation-based entry. Also sells clothes and souvenirs by local artists.

music_note

Live Hip-Hop & Breakdancing

Where: Plazas along the escalator route and at the top viewpoint areas. Performers set up at natural gathering points between escalator sections.

What to expect: Breakdancing (b-boys/b-girls), freestyle rappers, singers, poets. There are reportedly 1,000+ breakdancers in Comuna 13, including talented kids as young as 9–11.

Hip-hop’s role: Central to Comuna 13’s healing. Gave young people an alternative to gangs. The art form lets the community tell their stories.

Tipping: If you stop to watch, you should tip. 5,000–10,000 COP (~$1–2.50 USD) per performance. This is how many performers earn their living. Have small bills ready.

Local Art & Souvenir Shops

Prints of murals, hand-painted items, T-shirts, postcards, accessories, crafts. Many shops are just tables along the street–artists sell directly from their homes.

Family-run galleries: Many families run small galleries selling prints, crafts, and hand-painted items. Prices are fair and purchases directly support the community.

Budget: Small prints and postcards from 5,000–15,000 COP. T-shirts from 30,000–50,000 COP. Buy from local artists rather than chain shops to keep money in the community.

04 / Comuna 13 – History

Operación Orión & The Transformation

Understanding what happened here makes every mural, every breakdancer, every escalator ride infinitely more powerful. This is not ancient history–it was 2002.

Background

Comuna 13 (San Javier) is a hillside neighbourhood on the western slopes of the Aburrá Valley. Its strategic location made it a coveted corridor for armed groups.

Originally settled informally by displaced people fleeing rural violence, it became one of Medellín’s poorest and most marginalised neighbourhoods.

After Escobar’s death (1993): A power vacuum created a bloody battle between left-wing guerrillas (FARC, ELN), right-wing paramilitaries (AUC), and local street gangs, all fighting for control.

Operación Orión (16–17 October 2002)

Under President Álvaro Uribe’s orders, the Colombian military launched Operation Orion to root out guerrilla groups from Comuna 13.

Scale: ~1,500 personnel from the Army, Police, DAS, CTI, and Special Anti-Terrorist Forces. Supported by tanks and armed helicopters. Extended through November into December 2002.

Major controversy: Soldiers fought hand-in-hand with brutal AUC paramilitary groups.

auto_awesome The Transformation

02–10
Infrastructure & Social Investment

Medellín’s mayors invested heavily in Comuna 13 infrastructure, education, and social programmes. Community organisations worked alongside government to reclaim public space. Over 80% reduction in homicide rates.

2011
The Escalators

Installation of the escaleras eléctricas–a game-changing moment that physically reconnected the isolated hillside to the city. 6 escalators replaced 350 steps. World first.

2011+
Street Art Explosion & Casa Kolacho

Local artists began painting murals depicting the violence and its aftermath. Government encouraged this. Casa Kolacho founded by local hip-hop artists–offering dance, painting, music classes, and guided tours. The Graffitour was born.

Now
The Most Visited Site in Medellín

2024: Medellín recorded its lowest homicide rate in 82 years. The Graffitour is now the most visited site in the entire city. Murals serve dual purpose: memorials for the community AND economic drivers through tourism.

05 / Comuna 13 – Practical Tips & Getting There

Everything You Need Before You Go

checkroom

What to Wear

Shoes: Comfortable walking shoes with good grip–essential. Steep, hilly, uneven surfaces. You will walk uphill even with the escalators.

Rain jacket: Lightweight, packable. April = peak rainy season.

Clothing: Light, breathable. T-shirt and shorts/light trousers.

Avoid: Flashy jewellery, expensive watches, designer gear. Dress down. This is a working-class neighbourhood.

photo_camera

Photography Etiquette

Murals & street art: Fair game. Photograph freely.

People’s faces and homes: Always ask. “Puedo tomar una foto?” goes a long way.

Performers: Welcome photos/video–but tip them if you do.

Remember this is a living neighbourhood, not a theme park. People’s homes, laundry, and daily lives are right there.

shield

Safety

Main route: The escalator corridor is safe. Well-organised, monitored, constant presence of locals, artists, guides, and visitors.

Don’t: Wander far uphill or downhill on your own, especially off the escalator route.

After dark: Don’t visit without a guide.

Safe for solo travellers, couples, and groups. Families with children visit regularly. Consider wearing your backpack on your front in crowded areas.

schedule

Best Time to Visit

Morning (9–11 AM): Fewest crowds, drier weather, cooler.

Late afternoon (4–5 PM): Golden light hits murals beautifully. More mellow vibe. Higher rain risk.

Avoid: Midday heat (12–2 PM).

Your Tue 7 Apr slot: 2–5 PM works well. Bring a rain jacket just in case. Allow 3–4 hours with food stops, art shopping, and viewpoints.

✓ Book with community-run operators (Casa Kolacho, Zippy Tours)
✓ Buy from local artists and vendors rather than chain shops

directions Getting There from El Poblado

Option A: Metro Recommended

Route: Poblado → Line A (direction Niquia, 4 stops) → Change at San Antonio → Line B to last stop: San Javier.

Fare: 3,430 COP with Cívica card (~$0.82) or 3,900 COP without (~$0.93).

Time: ~25–30 minutes El Poblado to San Javier.

Cívica card: Free personalised card at San Javier, San Antonio, Niquia, or Itagui stations (Mon–Fri 6:30am–8:30pm, Sat 9:30am–4:30pm). Or 10,900 COP for rechargeable card at fare booths.

From San Javier to Comuna 13: 20–30 min walk uphill (safe, well-signposted), or green bus from outside the station (1,500–2,000 COP). Many tours meet at San Javier station.

Option B: Taxi / Uber / Didi

From El Poblado: ~7,000–15,000 COP (~$2–4 USD) via Uber/Didi/Cabify.

Metered taxi: Slightly more, ~10,000–20,000 COP.

Time: 20–35 minutes depending on traffic.

Tip: Use Uber/Didi rather than hailing a taxi for transparent pricing. The apps work well in Medellín.

06 / Late Afternoon – ~17:00

Parque Arví via MetroCable

From Comuna 13, Uber to Acevedo metro station and take MetroCable Line L up into the cloud forest. 16,000 hectares of Andean forest above the city with 54 km of trails, butterfly gardens, and the Mercado Arví artisan market. The cable car ride itself is a highlight – 20 minutes floating over green hillsides.

Open Tue–Sun • Closed Mon (unless holiday, then closed Tue)

Step 2 Line K cable car up to Santo Domingo (3 stops)
Step 3 Transfer to Line L at Santo Domingo → Arví station (~20 min)

The ride: Line L floats over the forest canopy with panoramic views of Medellín and the green hillsides of Santa Elena. One of the best cable car rides in South America.

Buy Line L tickets at Santo Domingo station before boarding.

Critical: Last cable car down is 6 PM. If you miss it, you’ll need a taxi back (~45 min drive, ~50,000 COP). Plan to be at the Arví station platform by 5:40 PM latest.

Mercado Arví

Right next to the Arví MetroCable station. Local farmers and vendors sell produce, cakes, coffee, jewellery, clothes, crafts and souvenirs.

Weekends: 40+ stands. Weekdays: ~10 stands. Bandeja paisa restaurant on weekends.

hiking

Hiking Trails

13 trails across 54 km. Most are low-difficulty, 1–4 hours. Maps available near the station.

Top pick: Camino Cieza de León (the “Pre-Hispanic Trail”) – a 1,500+ year old stone path with valley viewpoints. 2 hrs, low difficulty.

Guided tours: Depart every 30 min, 10 AM–4 PM. 50,000 COP (~$12) for foreigners.

pets

Biodiversity

70+ bird species, 72 butterfly species, 19 mammal species. Native orchids throughout.

Piedras Blancas: Separate eco-park with butterfly farm (20 species), insect museum (15,000+ specimens), lake with row boats, trails. Entry 20,700 COP.

Comfama Adventure Park

Ropes courses in the trees (4 circuits, 45–90 min), zip line at 30m height, 4D cinema, interactive mining exhibit.

Price: 3,000–22,700 COP. Bus from Arví station every 30 min on weekends. Open until 5 PM.

Café Arví: Colombian single-origin coffee in the Environmental & Cultural Centre.

El Tambo Restaurante: Down the road from the station. Big portions of typical Colombian food – fish, meats, rice, beans, arepas.

Mercado Arví: Street food and snacks at the market stalls.

directions_bike

Bikes & Horses

Horses: 30,000 COP / 30 min or 60,000 COP / 1 hr. Only go with someone who knows the park.

Bicycles: Available to rent. Reserve online in advance. Great way to cover the massive park.

tips_and_updates Practical Tips

Temperature: ~10°F / 5–6°C cooler than Medellín. Bring a jacket or sweater – it’s cloud forest elevation.

What to bring: Rain jacket/umbrella (April = wet), comfortable shoes/trainers, sunscreen (stronger sun at altitude despite cooler air).

Budget 3–4 hours minimum to explore trails, market, and grab coffee.

Safety: Stick to trails with other people. Don’t wander alone into remote sections. Weekends are busier and safer. Guided tours recommended.

Park entry: Free. You only pay for the cable car, guided tours, and Piedras Blancas/Comfama if you visit those.

Your Tue 7 Apr slot: Tuesday is fine (park open Tue–Sun). Arriving ~5 PM means you’ll have about an hour – enough for the market, a short trail, and coffee, but not a long hike. Consider arriving earlier if the schedule allows.
