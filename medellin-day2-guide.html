02 / Escobar Tour – Key Stops

What You’ll See

Each stop tells a chapter of the story. The best tours connect them into a narrative about how violence reshaped a city–and how that city fought back.

park

Parque Inflexión

Formerly the Monaco Building • El Poblado

Escobar’s luxury apartment tower–a symbol of narco excess. In 1988, the Cali Cartel detonated a car bomb outside targeting his family. The building was demolished by the city and replaced with a 5,160 sq metre memorial park.

What’s there now: 80% green space. A 70-metre black stone wall with 46,612 holes–one for each victim of narcoterrorism (1983–1994). A “Forest of Resilience” with trees from across Colombia. Free entry. Allow 20–30 minutes.

roofing

Rooftop Where Escobar Died

Los Olivos • Carrera 79B #45D-94

On 2 December 1993, the Search Bloc tracked Escobar to this house. He was shot fleeing across the roof, aged 44. Now a Spanish language school.

Near: Estadio / Santa Lucia metro. Quiet residential neighbourhood. Be respectful–this is someone’s workplace and a regular street.

house

Barrio Pablo Escobar

443 homes • Not on official city maps

443 homes Escobar built for low-income families in the 1980s. Many residents still view him as a benefactor. The complexity is real–your guide will walk you through it.

Community benefit: Tour proceeds fund a local foundation teaching English and technology to children. Ask before photographing residents.

deceased

Cementerio Jardines Montesacro

Calle 14 #52-52, Itagui • Free entry

Family plot near the southwest corner of the chapel. Contains Pablo, his mother, and bodyguard “El Limón” (killed in the same rooftop shootout). Hillside location with valley views.

Behaviour: Active cemetery–be quiet, respectful. No celebratory selfies. You may encounter funerals. 7–10 min walk from Sabaneta metro station (Line A south).

domain

La Catedral (Escobar’s Self-Built Prison)

Hilltop above Envigado • 1.5–2 hrs at site

In 1991, Escobar negotiated surrender and was confined to a “prison” he designed: jacuzzi, football pitch, bar/nightclub, doll house, waterfall. He continued running the cartel from inside. Escaped July 1992.

Now mostly ruins. Guides walk you through cells, nightclub, guard tower, tunnels, football pitch, escape route, and helipad with panoramic city views. Since June 2025, some areas restricted.

Note: Not all tours include La Catedral. Medellín Up Close does. Transport included, departs from Parque Lleras, El Poblado.

museum

Museo Casa de la Memoria

Free • Parque Bicentenario • The ethical counterweight

FREE multimedia museum honouring violence victims. Interactive maps, testimonies, art installations. The ethical counterpart to the Escobar family museum. Highly recommended.

Hours Tue–Fri 9AM–6PM, Sat–Sun 10AM–4PM
Location Parque Bicentenario, La Candelaria
Entry Free

Could fit on Day 4 if not included in the Day 3 tour itinerary.

warning

Casa Museo Pablo Escobar – Ethical Note

Run by Roberto Escobar Gaviria (Pablo’s brother) and his nephew. Contains Escobar’s motorcycles, classic cars, a plane, helicopter, and a gun allegedly from Al Capone. Fee: ~$30 USD (COP 120,000) or ~$47 USD for a guided tour. Money goes directly to the Escobar family. The narrative is told from their sympathetic perspective. Many ethical tourism advocates recommend skipping this and visiting the Museo Casa de la Memoria instead.

03 / Escobar Tour – Historical Context

The Medellín Cartel Era

Context that makes the tour more meaningful. Your guide will cover most of this, but understanding the scale helps.

Key Timeline

70s
Medellín Cartel Formed

Escobar, Carlos Lehder, Gonzalo Rodríguez Gacha, and the Ochoa brothers create the cartel.

82
Escobar Elected to Colombian Congress

Later expelled when criminal activities exposed. At peak, cartel controls ~80–90% of US cocaine supply, revenue ~$100 million/day, Escobar’s net worth ~$30 billion.

89
Avianca Flight 203 Bombed

27 Nov 1989. Boeing 727 destroyed 5 minutes after takeoff from Bogotá. All 107 on board + 3 on ground killed. Target was presidential candidate César Gaviria, who changed plans and survived. 10 days later: DAS headquarters bombed in Bogotá–63 killed, 600+ wounded.

91
Peak Violence & Surrender

Murder rate: 381 per 100,000 (~19 homicides/day). Nearly 40x UN endemic violence threshold. Over 500 police officers murdered in 1990–91. 46,612 people killed during the narcoterrorism era. 4,766 children among the dead. June: Escobar surrenders, enters La Catedral.

92
Escapes La Catedral

July 1992. Government tries to transfer him. He flees through the tunnels he had built. 16-month manhunt begins.

93
Shot Dead on a Rooftop

2 December 1993. Los Olivos neighbourhood. Aged 44. Cartel fragments and ceases to exist.

trending_up Medellín’s Transformation Since

95%

Decrease in murder rate. From 381/100k (1991) to ~20/100k (2015).

66%

Decrease in extreme poverty. Only 2.8% by 2017.

2013

“Innovative City of the Year” (WSJ), beating New York and Tel Aviv.

2024

Lowest homicide rate in 82 years. Lee Kuan Yew World City Prize (2016).

Metrocable connects hillside comunas to city centre (30,000 passengers/day). Biblioteca España, Parque Arví, integrated public transport–all addressing root causes of violence.

04 / Escobar Tour – Ethics & Practical Tips

Doing It Right

balance Ethical Considerations

The problem: Narco-tourism transforms criminal legacies into consumable spectacles, glamorising drug lords while omitting victims. Many Colombians deeply resent Escobar defining their national identity.

The Netflix effect: Narcos (2015) dramatically increased demand, often attracting visitors more interested in glamour than tragedy.

The complexity: In Barrio Pablo Escobar, residents still view him as a benefactor. Local perspectives are not monolithic.

✓ Choose operators that centre victims’ stories, not Escobar’s “empire”

✓ Visit the Museo Casa de la Memoria (free)–the ethical counterweight

✗ Avoid the Escobar family museum if possible (money goes to the family)

✗ Do NOT buy Escobar merchandise–many locals find it deeply offensive

✗ No celebratory selfies at grave sites or crime scenes

tips_and_updates Practical Tips

Wear: Comfortable walking shoes (uneven terrain at La Catedral ruins), light breathable clothes (22–28°C in April), hat, sunglasses, sunscreen.

Bring: Small backpack/crossbody (not flashy), water bottle, cash in small COP bills for tips and snacks, phone for photos but keep discreet.

Photography: Generally allowed at all major stops. Ask permission before photographing residents in Barrio Pablo Escobar. Be mindful of funerals at the cemetery.

Safety: Book with reputable operator. Don’t flash expensive items. “No dar papaya” (don’t give pickpockets an easy target). Stick with group and guide.

Timing: Morning start (8–9 AM) is best–cooler, better light, less crowded. Half-day (3–5 hrs) for standard tour.

Tipping: 15,000–30,000 COP / $4–7 USD / £3–6 pp for group tours. For private guides, 10–15% or 30,000–50,000 COP.

05 / Afternoon schedule ~13:00

Explore Laureles

Uber across the river to Medellín’s more local, increasingly hip neighbourhood. Carrera 70 (“La 70” or “La Setenta”) is the main strip – a completely different energy to El Poblado. Less polished, more authentic. This is the side of Medellín most tourists don’t see.

explore

Time Out #1 Coolest Neighbourhood (2023). Laureles topped the global list for its balance of energy and tranquility – tree-lined residential streets, family-owned restaurants, independent cafés, and a nightlife strip that locals have kept largely to themselves.

Where El Poblado caters to tourists, La 70 caters to paisas. Booze is cheaper, the music is louder, and the crowd is almost entirely Colombian. Thursday to Saturday the strip comes alive – tables spill onto pavements, aguardiente flows, and the salsa bars fill up by 10 PM.

Lunch on La 70

Pick one. All within a short walk of each other on or near Carrera 70.

Top Pick ~£7–13 pp

Bárbaro Cocina Primitiva

Cra 76 #73B-39, Laureles

Fire-grilled steaks, ribs, and slow-smoked cuts. “Primitive cooking” concept – open flames, wood smoke, generous portions. The Laureles original; there’s also a Poblado branch but this one has more character.

TripAdvisor 4.9/5 • 1,665 reviews • #5 in Medellín
Vibe Open-air, background music, calm
Opens 12:00 noon

Order: The costillas (ribs) and entraña (skirt steak) are the standouts. Staff make personalised recommendations. Expect 30–40 min wait on weekends.

Most Authentic ~£6–10 pp

Mondongo’s La 70

Cra 70 #Circular 3-43, Laureles

The Laureles branch of Medellín’s most iconic comida típica spot. Same huge bandeja paisa but fewer tourists than the Poblado location. Directly on La 70. More local feel. Good for the full Antioquian spread.

Rating 4.7/5 • 11,369 reviews (all branches)
Prices 21,000–36,000 COP per dish
Vibe Busy, family-run, enormous portions

Order: Bandeja paisa (the full spread: beans, rice, chicharrón, ground meat, egg, avocado, arepa, plantain) or the mondongo soup (tripe stew). 13 traditional dishes on the menu.

Lighter Option ~£5–9 pp

Olivia Pizzeria

Av 74B #39-46, near La 70

Thin-crust wood-fired pizza and fresh pasta. Good cocktails and sangria. Lighter option if you don’t want heavy Colombian food after a heavy morning tour. Airy, bright interior.

TripAdvisor 4.5/5 • Popular local chain (8 locations)
Menu 29 pizza varieties, pasta, cocktails
Best for If the group wants something lighter

Try: The mushroom pizza (mozzarella, parmesan, mushroom mix, truffle oil) or cinque formaggi. Shared antipasto board is a good starter.

06 / Afternoon fork_right ~16:00

Pick Your Afternoon

After lunch, pick one. Both paths end up back in El Poblado by evening.

nightlife

La 70 Drinks

Stay in Laureles • Hop between bars • Uber back for Bar Carmen

La 70 comes alive as the sun drops. The strip fills with paisas, the aguardiente starts flowing, and by 9 PM you’re dancing on tables. Hop between these three, then Uber back to El Poblado.

La Tienda de la 70

~£2–5 / drink

THE bar on La 70. Full-on Colombian party. “Fonda” decor with balloons, flashing lights, colourful beads dangling from the ceiling, and a giant pig. Rounds of aguardiente, dancing on tables. Gets lively from 8–9 PM.

Vibe Loud, bright, quintessentially paisa
Food Bandeja paisa, picadas, grilled snacks
Cover Free (cover only for special events)
Best nights Thu–Sat. Live acts on weekends

Expect: This is not a “cocktail bar” experience. It’s rounds of aguardiente, table-dancing, and live acts. The most Colombian night out you can have.

La Charcu 70

~£2–6 / drink

Craft beer and cocktails on La 70. More laid back – good to start the evening before things ramp up. Lively but not chaotic; mellow enough for actual conversation.

Address Cra 70 #Circular 2-48, Laureles
Menu Craft beers, cocktails, nachos, empanadas, tapas
Crowd Mix of locals and foreigners

Son Havana

~£2–4 / drink

Iconic salsa bar with live bands. Cuban-themed, packed dance floor. Founded in 2010, inspired by Cuban musical tradition. The house band – Son Havana All Stars – play Timba (a high-energy subgenre of salsa).

Location Cra 73 x Calle 44 (San Juan), near La 70
Live music At least 1–2x/week; Fri = salsa lessons + mojitos
Mon opening Check ahead: +57 320 451 2267

Tip: You don’t need to know salsa. The energy is infectious and the crowd is forgiving. Wednesday = Casino/Rueda group salsa classes. Friday = lessons + cold mojitos.

tips_and_updates Practical Notes

Schedule: Daily 9 AM – 5 PM, weather permitting. Book via WhatsApp – super easy to message and lock in a slot.

GoPro: Most operators mount a GoPro to the pilot’s harness. Footage usually available for purchase afterwards (often included in higher-tier packages).

Late afternoon: Not a formal “sunset slot” but booking 3–4 PM gives you golden-hour light over the valley. Weather is typically calmer in the afternoon.

Landing: You land in a field near El Poblado – short Uber back to the hotel.
